Will Robots Take Over Our Jobs?

presented by Andre Bryant N. Bagalso

STS 1 Final Task

Note that "robots" is quite a broad term. Throughout this essay, robots can be referred to as artificial intelligence (AI), automation tools, mechanical technologies, or machine learning. This webpage was made by Andre Bryant N. Bagalso for the final task of STS 1.

Introduction

Of course, it is practical to automate repetitive tasks. In the context of my field, software testing is often automated, reducing the need for 100% human involvement. This approach is faster, more efficient, and minimizes errors. However, for non-functional testing (testing user experience), it is often necessary to rely on humans. This is because robots fall short with this type of testing. While scientific studies outline principles of aesthetics, AI can only offer suggestions. The subjective nature of human experiences, creativity, and intuition remains irreplaceable.


This opens up the question: will robots take over our jobs? The answer is NO.


According to Rico (2023), technological advancements have replaced certain jobs but simultaneously created new opportunities. We can see this in the history of industries. For example, when manual billboard painting (e.g. Hand-Painted Movie Billboards) was replaced by automated printing, the painting job was indeed lost. However, industries thrived with new roles such as graphic designers, printer manufacturers, and creative developers. Eventually, even with the rise of digital billboards (e.g. the 3D buillboard in Shinjuku, Japan) gave rise to even more jobs such as 3D designers, animators, and artists. Similarly, automation in factories displaced manual labor but gave rise to machinery production and maintenance jobs.


Argument 1: Robots only take on repetitive tasks.


AI and robots are designed to handle tasks that follow clear and repetitive patterns, such as data entry, quality checks, and manufacturing processes. These tasks are often tedious and prone to human error, making automation a practical choice. However, when it comes to jobs that need creativity, cultural understanding, or emotional intelligence, machines fall short. For instance, while an AI tool can recommend design improvements based on trends, it cannot replicate the originality and personal touch of a human designer.

Similarly, roles like caregiving, teaching, or customer service rely on empathy and personal connectionsâ€”qualities that robots cannot provide. Even in industries where AI is heavily used, such as healthcare or marketing, humans remain essential for problem-solving and decision-making. Rather than replacing humans, robots complement human capabilities by taking over repetitive tasks and freeing people to focus on jobs that require creativity and emotional depth.

Argument 2: With the rise of technology, more jobs also open.


The rise of AI and technology is not just about replacing jobs; it also creates entirely new opportunities. Roles like AI ethics consultants, machine learning engineers, and data scientists have emerged as businesses adopt advanced technologies. These careers didn't exist a few decades ago but are now in high demand. They show how technology expands the job market by introducing new fields and reshaping industries.

Education and training systems play an important role in helping workers adapt to these changes. By learning skills in programming, data analysis, and AI development, people can prepare for the growing demand in these areas. This evolution of the job market demonstrates that technology doesn't just take away jobs, it also creates exciting new ones, paving the way for future opportunities.

Conclusion & Uncertainty with the future with impending AI replacements

It's true that AI and robots will change how industries work and may take over some jobs. However, this doesn't mean humans will be left without work. Throughout history, people have adapted to big changes, finding new and meaningful roles. While AI handles repetitive tasks, humans focus on creativity, problem-solving, and ideas that machines cannot replicate.

As future programmers, we have a key role in this shift. It's our job to create systems that work with people rather than replace them. By doing so, we can ensure that technology helps us grow and opens up new opportunities instead of becoming a threat to jobs.
